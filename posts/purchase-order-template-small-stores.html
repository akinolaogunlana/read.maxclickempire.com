Google Docs Purchase Order Template for Small Stores

If you run a small store, every dollar counts—and so does every document. A purchase order (PO) isn’t just paperwork. It’s your frontline tool for keeping inventory under control, avoiding miscommunication with suppliers, and tracking expenses. Google Docs offers a flexible and user-friendly way to create, customize, and reuse purchase orders without paying for expensive software. This guide walks you through everything you need to know to get started with a purchase order template in Google Docs, built for the real needs of small retail shops.

Why Small Stores Need Purchase Orders

Whether you own a corner convenience store, a boutique, or a local hardware shop, managing your supplies is non-negotiable. A purchase order protects both buyer and seller by outlining exactly what’s being purchased and under what terms. Here’s why it matters:

  • Clarity with suppliers: A PO ensures there’s no confusion about items, quantities, and prices.
  • Tracking inventory: It helps you monitor what’s ordered versus what’s received.
  • Expense control: You can forecast costs and avoid surprise invoices.
  • Record-keeping: POs create a paper trail that simplifies accounting and taxes.

What Should a Purchase Order Include?

A functional PO doesn’t have to be complicated, but it must be complete. Your template should always include these essential elements:

  1. Purchase order number: A unique reference for tracking.
  2. Date of order: When the PO is issued.
  3. Supplier details: Business name, address, and contact info.
  4. Buyer details: Your store's name and contact information.
  5. Item description: Product names, SKUs, or brief details.
  6. Quantities and unit prices: Clear pricing for transparency.
  7. Subtotal and taxes: Any applicable charges.
  8. Delivery instructions: Shipping method, address, or deadline.
  9. Authorized signature: Approval from your side, if needed.

How to Use a Google Docs Purchase Order Template

Google Docs allows you to create and edit documents from anywhere, on any device. Here’s how to use a PO template step-by-step:

Step 1: Open Google Docs and Search for Templates

Start by going to docs.google.com. In the main dashboard, click on “Template gallery” if available, or create a new blank document. You can also download a PO template from trusted sources (see links below) and open it with Google Docs.

Step 2: Customize the Header

Edit the top section of the template with your store’s logo, name, address, and contact details. If you don’t have a logo, just use bold text for the business name and make sure your address is clear and accurate.

Step 3: Fill in Supplier Information

In the designated area, input your vendor’s business name, phone number, and address. Double-check spelling and formatting—it helps when you need to reorder or track shipments later.

Step 4: Input Order Details

Fill out the itemized table with the following:

  • Product names or codes
  • Quantities needed
  • Unit prices
  • Line totals

The template should auto-calculate the total cost if built with formulas. If not, use Google Sheets embedded into Google Docs or manually enter totals with a calculator.

Step 5: Add Delivery Instructions and Notes

If the shipment has special requirements—like delivery by a specific date or to a specific dock—add those in the comments or footer section of the template. Also include return policy notes if needed.

Step 6: Save and Share

Once complete, go to File > Download to save it as a PDF for emailing. Or click Share to send it directly to your supplier using their email address. You can also keep a copy in your Google Drive folder dedicated to purchases.

Tips for Making the Most of Your Template

Once you’ve got your PO template working, turn it into a long-term tool with these tips:

  • Duplicate and rename: Each time you issue a new PO, make a copy and rename it using the PO number and date.
  • Create a PO log: Use Google Sheets to track all your orders in one place, linking back to each PO in Docs.
  • Use consistent formatting: Keep fonts, colors, and layout consistent for professionalism.
  • Set permissions: If you have staff, control who can edit or view the purchase orders by setting proper sharing settings.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Can I use Google Docs to create legally binding purchase orders?

A: Yes, a properly formatted PO created in Google Docs can be legally binding when accepted by a supplier. Ensure both parties have access and agreement on the terms.

Q: Is there a free purchase order template for small businesses?

A: Absolutely. Many websites offer downloadable PO templates for free. You can open these in Google Docs and edit them as needed (see resource links below).

Q: Can I automate purchase order numbers in Google Docs?

A: Google Docs alone doesn’t support automation, but pairing it with Google Sheets allows you to auto-generate PO numbers using basic formulas and concatenate functions.

Q: What’s the best way to store POs for a small retail business?

A: Use Google Drive to create a folder system by vendor or month. Name files clearly using the PO number and date for easy retrieval.

Q: Should I print purchase orders or send them digitally?

A: For speed and efficiency, digital delivery is preferred. However, printing might be necessary for local suppliers who require hard copies or delivery drivers who need paperwork.
