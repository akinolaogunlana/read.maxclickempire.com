Short Story Structure Template in Google Docs

Every short story thrives on precise structure, engaging characters, and purposeful pacing. This article delivers a ready-to-use template for Google Docs, guiding authors through setup, plot beats, character dynamics, and scene layout with clarity and purpose.

Why a Structured Template Works for Short Stories

Short fiction demands economy and impact. A defined layout ensures you hit key narrative beats without wasted words. Frameworks like the three-act approach or Larry Brooks’s seven‑beat model give your story direction and emotional momentum .

Core Models Behind This Template

  • Three‑Act Structure: Sets up the world, tension, and resolution in a familiar arc .
  • Larry Brooks’s Seven Beats: Offers clear setup, shifts, midpoint, opposition, and resolution for short fiction rhythm.
  • Hero’s Journey / Story Circle: Scalable to short form with emotional transformation at its core.

Google Docs Template Layout

1. Title & Metadata

Give your working title, word target, date, and brief one‑line premise.

2. Act 1 – Opening & Inciting Incident

  • Ordinary World: Who is the protagonist and what is their status quo?
  • Inciting Incident: What moment disrupts their normal life?
  • Goal & Motivation: What does the character want—and why?

3. Act 2 – Conflict, Shifts & Midpoint

  • First Shift: Emotional or situational reaction to the inciting moment.
  • Complications: Rising obstacles and escalating tension.
  • Midpoint Shift: A key turning point changing stakes or perspective.

4. Act 2 Continued – Opposition & Escalation

  • Opposition/Resistance: Forces pushing back, testing the protagonist.
  • Internal Conflict: Emotional stakes and internal dilemmas.

5. Act 3 – Climax & Resolution

  • Climax: The decisive confrontation or change.
  • Resolution: Consequence, shift into new normal.
  • Final Image: Last line or scene evoking emotional closure.

6. Character Snapshot

Document name, internal desire vs. deeper need, flaw, and transformation.

7. Scene Breakdown

  1. Scene number
  2. Location / Time
  3. Purpose (what shifts or reveals)
  4. Word‑count goal

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: How long should each beat or act be in a short story?

A: That depends on your total length. For a ~3,000‑word piece, Act 1 might be ~500 w, mid ~1,500 w, Act 3 ~1,000 w, with shifts and midpoint anchors around one‑third intervals.

Q: Can I adapt the template to flash fiction?

A: Yes—simply collapse the beats. For flash, focus on a vivid setup, a sharp shift, and a resonant ending. Trace only two or three micro‑beats.

Q: Should I complete the whole template before drafting?

A: Not necessarily. Many find it more productive to fill in later sections as the story evolves, using the outline as a guide rather than a prescription.

Q: Is this compatible with more advanced beat sheets like Save the Cat or Story Circle?

A: Absolutely. You can embed additional beat sheet sections within the acts—e.g. Save the Cat’s fifteen beats or Harmon’s eight stages—while maintaining the core layout as your backbone .

Q: How do I track editing progress?

A: Use comments in Google Docs to mark sections that need revision or expansion, and collapse completed headings to keep focus. This structure doubles as a workflow board.
